üìä OPERATIONS ON FUNCTIONS üî¢

üéØ Key Concepts:
- **Operations on Functions**: Just like numbers, functions can be combined through various operations.

- **Basic Function Operations**:
1Ô∏è‚É£ **Addition**: \( (f + g)(x) = f(x) + g(x) \)
2Ô∏è‚É£ **Subtraction**: \( (f - g)(x) = f(x) - g(x) \)
3Ô∏è‚É£ **Multiplication**: \( (f \cdot g)(x) = f(x) \cdot g(x) \)
4Ô∏è‚É£ **Division**: \( \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)(x) = \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \), where \( g(x) \neq 0 \).

- **Function Composition**:
- \( (f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)) \), meaning the output of \( g(x) \) is used as the input of \( f(x) \).
- Order matters: \( f(g(x)) \neq g(f(x)) \) in most cases.

üìù Supporting Evidence:
- **Algebraic Properties**: Functions follow arithmetic rules when combined through addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division.
- **Graphical Analysis**: Graphs of combined functions illustrate their interaction visually.

‚úÖ Key Discussion Points:
- What happens when two functions are added, subtracted, multiplied, or divided?
- How does function composition differ from basic operations?
- Why is \( g(x) \neq 0 \) required in function division?

üåü Key Takeaways:
- Understanding function operations helps in analyzing real-world relationships between different variables.

üîÑ INVERSE FUNCTIONS üîÑ

üéØ Key Concepts:
- **Inverse Function Definition**: The inverse of a function reverses the input-output relationship. If \( f(x) \) maps \( x \) to \( y \), then the inverse function \( f^{-1}(x) \) maps \( y \) back to \( x \).

- **One-to-One Functions**: A function must be one-to-one (pass the horizontal line test) to have an inverse that is also a function.

- **Finding the Inverse of a Function**:
1Ô∏è‚É£ Replace \( f(x) \) with \( y \).
2Ô∏è‚É£ Swap \( x \) and \( y \).
3Ô∏è‚É£ Solve for \( y \) in terms of \( x \).
4Ô∏è‚É£ Replace \( y \) with \( f^{-1}(x) \).

- **Graphical Interpretation**: The graph of an inverse function is the reflection of the original function across the line \( y = x \).

üìù Supporting Evidence:
- **Horizontal Line Test**: A function has an inverse function if and only if it is one-to-one.
- **Algebraic Verification**: If \( f(f^{-1}(x)) = x \) and \( f^{-1}(f(x)) = x \), then the functions are true inverses.

‚úÖ Key Discussion Points:
- Why do some functions not have inverses?
- How can restricting the domain allow non-one-to-one functions to have an inverse?
- What real-world scenarios involve inverse functions?

üß† Quiz & Reflection:
- Find the inverse of \( f(x) = 3x + 5 \).
- Does \( f(x) = x^2 \) have an inverse? If not, how can we modify its domain?
- Graph \( f(x) = \frac{x - 2}{3} \) and its inverse on the same coordinate plane.

üåü Key Takeaways:
- The inverse of a function swaps inputs and outputs.
- Not all functions have inverses unless they are one-to-one.
- Graphing helps visualize the inverse as a reflection over \( y = x \).
