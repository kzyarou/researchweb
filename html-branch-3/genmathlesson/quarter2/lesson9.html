üìñ LOGARITHMIC FUNCTIONS: GRAPHING, PROPERTIES & APPLICATIONS üìä

üéØ Key Concepts:
- **Logarithmic Function**: The inverse of an exponential function, given by: \[ f(x) = \log_b(x) \] where \( b \) is the base and must be greater than 0 but not equal to 1.

- **Graphing Logarithmic Functions**: - The graph passes through \( (1,0) \), as \( \log_b(1) = 0 \). - It has a vertical asymptote at \( x = 0 \). - If \( b > 1 \), the function is increasing. - If \( 0 < b < 1 \), the function is decreasing.

- **Properties of Logarithms**: 1Ô∏è‚É£ **Product Rule**: \(\log_b(xy) = \log_b x + \log_b y\)
2Ô∏è‚É£ **Quotient Rule**: \(\log_b \left(\frac{x}{y}\right) = \log_b x - \log_b y\)
3Ô∏è‚É£ **Power Rule**: \(\log_b (x^n) = n \log_b x\)

- **Real-World Applications**: - Measuring **earthquake intensity** using the Richter scale. - Determining **pH levels** in chemistry. - Understanding **exponential growth and decay**, such as population models and radioactive decay.

‚úÖ Key Discussion Points:
- Why does the logarithmic function have a vertical asymptote at \( x = 0 \)?
- How does changing the base \( b \) affect the shape of the logarithmic graph?

üß† Quiz & Reflection:
- Sketch the graph of \( f(x) = \log_2(x) \) and describe its key features.
- Compare the graphs of \( \log_2(x) \) and \( \log_{1/2}(x) \).
- Explain why logarithms are used in measuring earthquake intensity.

üåü Key Takeaways:
- Logarithmic functions are the inverse of exponential functions and have distinct properties and behaviors.
- Their graphs contain a vertical asymptote and can either increase or decrease depending on the base.
- Logarithms play a crucial role in real-world applications, from science to finance.

üìñ TRANSFORMATIONS OF FUNCTIONS üìä

üéØ Key Concepts:
- **Function Transformation**: Modifications made to a function that shift, reflect, stretch, or compress its graph.

- **Types of Transformations**: 1Ô∏è‚É£ **Translations**: Shifting the graph without changing its shape.
- \( f(x) + k \) ‚Üí Vertical shift **up** by \( k \).
- \( f(x) - k \) ‚Üí Vertical shift **down** by \( k \).
- \( f(x - h) \) ‚Üí Horizontal shift **right** by \( h \).
- \( f(x + h) \) ‚Üí Horizontal shift **left** by \( h \).

2Ô∏è‚É£ **Reflections**: Flipping the graph over an axis.
- \( -f(x) \) ‚Üí Reflection across the **x-axis**.
- \( f(-x) \) ‚Üí Reflection across the **y-axis**.

3Ô∏è‚É£ **Stretches and Compressions**:
- **Vertical stretch**: \( a \cdot f(x) \), where \( |a| > 1 \) stretches the graph vertically.
- **Vertical compression**: \( a \cdot f(x) \), where \( 0 < |a| < 1 \) compresses the graph vertically.
- **Horizontal stretch**: \( f(bx) \), where \( 0 < |b| < 1 \) stretches the graph horizontally.
- **Horizontal compression**: \( f(bx) \), where \( |b| > 1 \) compresses the graph horizontally.

‚úÖ Key Discussion Points:
- How do different values of \( h \) and \( k \) affect the position of a function's graph?
- What is the relationship between stretching and compressing a function vertically and horizontally?

üåü Key Takeaways:
- Transformations allow us to manipulate graphs in predictable ways.
- Understanding transformations helps in graphing functions more efficiently.
