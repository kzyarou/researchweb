Compound Interest | General Mathematics

This video explains the concept of compound interest, the formula used to calculate it, and how it differs from simple interest in financial applications.

VIEWS: 12K LIKES: 128

üí∞ UNDERSTANDING COMPOUND INTEREST üìà

üéØ Key Concepts:
- **Compound Interest**: Interest calculated on the initial principal and also on the accumulated interest from previous periods.

- **Formula for Compound Interest**:
\[ A = P(1 + \frac{r}{n})^{nt} \] where:
- \( A \) = Final amount
- \( P \) = Principal (initial investment)
- \( r \) = Annual interest rate (decimal form)
- \( n \) = Number of times interest is compounded per year
- \( t \) = Time in years

- **Comparison with Simple Interest**:
- **Simple Interest**: Interest is only calculated on the principal: \( SI = P \times r \times t \).
- **Compound Interest**: Interest is earned on both the principal and previously accumulated interest, leading to exponential growth.

- **Effect of Compounding Frequency**:
- The more frequently interest is compounded, the greater the final amount.
- Common compounding periods: annually, semiannually, quarterly, monthly, and daily.

üìù Supporting Evidence:
- **Financial Growth**: Investments using compound interest grow faster than those using simple interest.
- **Applications**: Used in savings accounts, loans, investments, and retirement plans.

‚úÖ Key Discussion Points:
- How does the compounding period affect the total interest earned?
- Why do banks and financial institutions prefer compound interest over simple interest?

üß† Quiz & Reflection:
- If you invest $1,000 at an annual interest rate of 5% compounded quarterly, how much will you have after 5 years?
- Compare the final amount of an investment using simple vs. compound interest over 10 years.

üåü Key Takeaways:
- Compound interest allows money to grow exponentially over time.
- The frequency of compounding significantly impacts the final amount.

üí∞ UNDERSTANDING PRESENT VALUE & FUTURE VALUE üìà

üéØ Key Concepts:
- **Future Value (FV)**: The value of an investment after earning interest over a specific period.
- **Present Value (PV)**: The current worth of a future sum of money, discounted at a given interest rate.

- **Formula for Future Value**:
\[ FV = P(1 + r)^t \] where:
- \( FV \) = Future value
- \( P \) = Principal amount
- \( r \) = Interest rate (decimal form)
- \( t \) = Time in years

- **Formula for Present Value**:
\[ PV = \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^t} \] where:
- \( PV \) = Present value
- \( FV \) = Future value
- \( r \) = Interest rate (decimal form)
- \( t \) = Time in years

- **Time Value of Money**:
- Money today is worth more than the same amount in the future due to potential earning capacity.
- Interest rates and inflation affect the value of money over time.

üìù Supporting Evidence:
- **Investments**: Understanding PV and FV helps in making better investment decisions.
- **Loan Payments**: Used to determine how much needs to be saved today for a future financial goal.

‚úÖ Key Discussion Points:
- Why does money lose value over time?
- How do different interest rates impact PV and FV calculations?

üß† Quiz & Reflection:
- If you invest $5,000 at an annual interest rate of 6% for 5 years, what will be its future value?
- If you need $10,000 in 8 years and the interest rate is 4% per year, how much should you invest today?

üåü Key Takeaways:
- The **future value** of money grows due to interest over time.
- The **present value** helps determine how much money is needed today to reach a financial goal in the future.
